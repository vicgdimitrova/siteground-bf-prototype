Black Friday Campaign — Design Rationale

A short walkthrough of the thinking behind the campaign: how each piece carries the offer, and how the bold, festive look still reads as SiteGround.

Approach & inspiration

Before I started designing, I went back through SiteGround’s past Black Friday campaigns to see how the brand usually handles the season. The same approach kept coming up: bold layouts on dark, themed backgrounds, lifted by punchy accent colours that aren’t part of the everyday palette. I wanted my work to sit comfortably next to those, so I leaned into the same direction. The goal was to make this feel like the next SiteGround Black Friday, not something that came out of nowhere.

The visual foundation

Everything in the campaign is built on a background I made myself in Photoshop. I drew the shapes by hand and then worked them into a texture that feels dynamic and current. To keep the set varied without it falling apart, I used one consistent style across all of it, but gave each surface its own version of the image. That way the pages and assets clearly belong together, while none of them feels like a copy of the last. I was after something artistic but still clean, never busy.

Staying true to SiteGround

A themed campaign still has to hold on to the brand, so I was careful about what stayed fixed. The logo and the Poppins typography are untouched. For the accent, I went with a green/turquoise that sits close to one of SiteGround’s brand greens, and used it for the promo announcement bar, the main messaging, the labels and the buttons. That one colour carries a lot of the connection. It brings the Black Friday energy while keeping everything tied back to SiteGround, so the campaign and the regular site still feel like the same brand.

85% as the hero

The brief is clear that the discount is the most important message, so I made the 85% the lead visual on every piece. I gave it a mirror effect for two reasons. It’s a strong, current design trend, and it’s also something SiteGround already uses on its own site, which keeps the campaign in step with the live brand instead of pulling away from it. The percentage shows up on the index hero, the sales page, the email and every banner, so wherever someone runs into the campaign, the offer is the first thing they see.

Across the components

Index page. The hero leads with the offer itself: a big “85% Off Premium Hosting | From $1.99/mo”, the promo price next to the crossed-out regular one, a small, low-key “Excl. VAT” note, and a couple of urgency cues like the announcement bar and the “Now 85% Off” label. Further down, I kept SiteGround’s usual product overview but reskinned it in the theme, so the home page stays familiar even though it’s clearly on sale.

WordPress sales page. The header repeats the same “85% Off WordPress Hosting | From $1.99/mo” line. All three plan cards (StartUp, GrowBig as Best Value, and GoGeek) show the regular price crossed out next to the promo price, a Save 85% badge, the renewal note and the VAT line. The original layout is all still there, just dressed in the Black Friday look.

Email template. I built the email around the copy from the brief and kept it in the same visual world: a dark, eye-catching header with the 85% on it, the teal accents, and clear CTA buttons. It’s a single column so it holds up across the main email clients, and the key message, the price and the main button all sit in the top third, so the offer comes through even in a preview.

Banner set. Every size uses the same order of elements (logo, 85%, the offer and price, then a clear CTA), just squeezed to fit. On the smallest one, the 320×50, I cut it down to the essentials so the discount and the starting price still register at a glance.

The constants

A few things stay the same across the whole campaign. Both prices are always visible, with the promo price clearly in front. The VAT note is there but quiet. And the brand cues — the logo, Poppins and the green thread — hold the set together. The result is a campaign that feels bold and current for Black Friday but still reads as SiteGround the whole way through.
